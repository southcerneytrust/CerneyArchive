HADOW FAMILY

 

Rev W E Hadow, born 1826, Elstree Vicarage, Herts married Mary Lang Cornish, born 1833 in Tavistock, daughter of a lawyer.  They started their married life in the vicarage at Ebrington where the first five of their children were born, ie Henry, Margaret (later Mrs Cornish), Constance, Gerald and Edgecumbe.  Grace was born on 9 December 1875 in South Cerney vicarage (now Liddell House).  She was baptized in All Hallows on 6 January  1876 with her eldest brother, Harry later to be Sir W H Hadow, but then a boy of 16 as her godfather.

 

The Rev W E Hadow was a good preacher and read the scriptures well.  He was not noted as a practical person but was something of an authority on heraldry and brasses.  He took in pupils both at Ebrington and at South Cerney in order to supplement his stipend.

 

Grace went to Miss Ramsay’s school in Cirencester when she was twelve where she learnt German, French, Music and Drawing.  She did Arithmetic with the South Cerney village schoolmaster.  She had intermittent German governesses at home then won a scholarship to Brownshill Court School near Stroud where she stayed from the age of 13 to 15.  She attended school in Truro from 1891 to 1894 where she was head girl.  During this time her older sister, Margaret, married her cousin, John Cornish, who was a lawyer in Penzance.  In 1895 Grace went to Trier to learn German and music and returned to South Cerney in February 1896.

 

In 1899 her brother Gerald died of consumption in Penzance at the age of 28.  The family were stricken by his early death and there is a memorial to him in the parish church.  Later the same year Grace obtained a junior post at Cheltenham Ladies’ College.

In 1899 Henry Hadow became a member of Somerville College Council and her encouraged his sister to study in the Honour School of English Language and Literature, although she was unable to obtain a degree until 1922.

 

In 1906 when her father died she moved to Cirencester with her mother and sister, Constance.  They lived in Foss Lodge, ‘a squarish and uncompromising house of grey stone on the footpath of Sheep Street on the edge of the town and facing the station’.

 

From her she continued her studies and supported the cause of women’s education and became member of the Sub Committee of Agricultural Organisation  Society from which the WIs  were subsequently launched.
