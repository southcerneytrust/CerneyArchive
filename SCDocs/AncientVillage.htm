PREFACE

These records of South Cerney are gleaned from Doomsday‑book, Atkyns, Rudder, and Bigland’s Histories of Gloucestershire, a paper read to the Somerset Archaeological Society in 1936, and notes made during 45 years residence in South Cerney.

I am particularly indebted to the Rev.T. E. Johnson for the help he has given me, in the part about the Church, the editing, and correcting the manuscript. Also to the Rev. F. Kennen who made the excavations of the Castle site and his notes.

My thanks are also due to the Society of Antiquaries for their help and report on the Tympanum and the relic found in the wall of the Church.

It is hoped this small book may keep alive the history of this village for future generations.

As this book goes to press the death occurred of the Rev. T. E. Johnson much to the regret of his many friends in South Cerney

South Cerney

The village of South Cerney lies three miles South East of Cirencester. It is picturesque and typical of others in the vicinity, still fortunately possessing many gabled old stone houses with mullion windows and stone tiled roofs so dear to the hearts of dwellers in the Cotswold country.

It lies 345 feet above sea level with a gravel subsoil. With the adjoining hamlet of Cerney Wick, which is part of the Parish, it has a total population of 1,100.

We know a good deal of the history of the Church, which is full of interest and beauty, also of the three manor houses, thanks to Atkyns standard work on Gloucestershire, Domesday Book, and Rudder’s Gloucestershire, but little is known of the actual history of the village itself as no records have been kept.

The place takes its name from the Churn, a stream which runs through it. The ‘ey ‘ of Cerney, from the Saxon ‘ eye ‑water, means water or island indicating that the place was once a watery morass.From the Cotswold watershed the Churn, rising at Seven Springs, the principal source of the Thames, winds its serpentine way through Rendcombe, North Cerney and Cirencester, round to South Cerney, and so to Cricklade where it forms a tributary of the Thames. The water sometimes overflows its banks and these fields have been called ‘ water meadows.’ Their cultivation, keeping the ditches in repair, and flooding, called ‘drownding’ gave regular occupation to a number of people. Domesday Book records :‑“ its meadows and mills were of great value.” After rain or melting snow the water rushes with speed over the little weir and under the bridge. In some of the earliest records or the district some fields are referred to as ‘famouns for lust grass and of great value in cattle grazing.

Crane Farm, S.E. of the Church, possessed admirable watercress beds for generations ‘When the R A F aerodrome was established at South Cerney in 1938 fossilized shell fish were unearthed in great quantities, as well as many flints, all of which were found in beds of Oolite and some moderate veins of clay. At a lower level, close to the South Cerney Cirencester road and below the railway embankment, Mr. J. Stait who was building a bungalow on the site, found, whilst sinking an 18ft. well, a fossilized fish about the size of a mackerel embedded in blue lias clay. So the great icefield which once crossed the area, as it melted, released treasures and left behind these fossils.

Gravel beds, crossed in several places by yellow and blue clay, cover acres of land to the South of the village and have yielded many thousands of tons of sand and gravel of various sizes from pits 20ft. deep. These pits immediately filled with water, and have now become a series of large ponds between the village and Wick. The gravel is yellow, and identical with the Oolite of the Cotswolds. These gravel pits were, centuries ago, the burial ground of people of whose identities we have no record, stone and lead coffins were found dating back to Saxon times.

Before the County and Parish boundaries were altered there were islands of one county in the adjoining one. There are many in our county belonging to Wiltshire at the present time. The Police in those days had jurisdiction only in their own county. Rumour has it that in the old days a part or the village on the South side of the stream was an island of Wiltshire, the open space in front of the Eliot Arms was reputed to be a favourite spot for the illegal sport of Cockfighting, the police could only stand on the Gloucestershire side of the bridge and enjoy the sport. Whether this is true cannot be substantiated as no trace in the old maps in the Record Office show such an island. Cockfighting in those days, whether it was legal or not, was a National Sport. One old inhabitant at that time was an expert in the art of cutting the cock’s combs and trimming them up for their battles, and had a considerable trade in the locality in this art.

Many of the old quaint customs have died out; there are still people alive who as children can remember seeing effigies of unfortunate people who were considered to be witches, and other who had gained notoriety for their misdeeds being carried round the village, and these effigies burnt. Happily those days are gone, but we still miss the old Christmas plays with the Cerney Mummers, for the village had a special mumming play of its own.

Sheep stealing was rife, the sheep being driven as quickly as possible over the Parish boundary to Ashton Keynes. The writer knew a man, 40 years ago, whose father worked in chains when convicted of this offence. These are interesting sidelights on the life of the village, more than a hundred years ago.

As already noted the village has no special place in history, but Doomsday Book tells us that just before Alfred was King (about AD. 870) one hundred acres of rich pasture land and three water mills were bestowed by Beor‑Wulf on his favourite Abbey of Abingdon. The rapacious Archbishop Stigand, seized it and held it for ten years until he was deposed.

In 1071 the Earl of Hereford held it and later it was given to Walter de Bohum. From Atkyns and Rudder we learn that early in the 12th century a castle was built by Hereditary Sherriff Milo FitzWalter, Earl of Hereford.  This castle was later taken by King Stephen and subsequently retaken in the first civil war.  When retaken by Milo the castle was made into one of a chain of posts from Malmesbury to Cricklade.  The following notes are by the Rev. P. Kennen, Vicar of the Parish 1928-45 who carried out considerable excavations on the Castle site which lies North West of The Limes House. 

“ The surface presents the appearance of Mound. Moat. and Enclosure. This idea, strengthened by the traditional name ‘ The Castle Ground,’ was confirmed by trenches cut through these mounds which revealed artificial construction of a wide wall, and by searching for a guard room at an obvious entrance, through the Inner Mound on the North side, foundation walls on three sides 4ft. to 6ft. deep were found for a room about 12ft. square.

From the South wall, which was badly broken probably by the fall of a superstructure, a passage way or trench led to the remains of a kitchen or cookhouse, where burnt earth, ashes, animal and bird bones were found. Rising some 2ft. was part of a paved floor, stones hollowed out for door posts, and wood ashes probably from fire when the building was demolished.

Following the lines of foundations, coins, pottery, and clay pipe stems were found. The coins were Charles the First farthings, the clay pipes were probably from Bristol. Both the latter were too near the surface and too late in date to be contemporary with the original fort, earthworks, etc.

The Castle was a military post, held in strength, by troops during the civil wars of Stephen and Matilda, and was probably demolished in the last half of the 12th century.

The fact that troops holding a defence post would require water suggested a search for a well, and the incident of a water‑diviner searching for a flow to The Limes gave a splendid opportunity. On his third cast within the moat an ancient square headed well was found about 2ft. below the turf. The stones were of good shape, the toppers having rectangular slabs. Silting had nearly filled up the well, the walls were only about 12ft. deep, but the silt was very full of water.

Though the line of forts was undoubtedly used by Stephen and Matilda in their fluctuating conflicts, it is highly probable that the stronger posts, of which South Cerney was one, were of earlier date. Note the proximity of Ermin Street, a Saxon community, and burial ground, (the present gravel pits), and the finding of Roman coins and ornaments in the present Vicarage garden, churchyard and R.A.F. aerodrome. If this surmise is right the earliest mounds of defence would be British to check Romans, or Saxons against Norman. Unfortunately a short time ago a valuable heifer was found in the well and had to be destroyed. The well was then filled in.”

 

 

THE THREE MANORS.

We learn that on the death of Milo Fitzwalter’s five grandsons, who were probably the descendants of the De Bohans, the estate was divided between the surviving three granddaughters, hence the three manors in the Parish.

ATKYNS MANOR

The largest in acreage. From 1234 it was held by the Barons of St. Amande for 400 years. They were probably descendants of Milo and also possibly kin to the de  Bohuns. In 1661 it was sold by Sir Nevil Poole of Oaksey (whose fortunes were wrecked by his loyalty to Charles I) to Sir Edward Atkyns, who left it to Sir Robert, his son, thence to his son Sir Robert Atkyns who sold off most of the manor to divers freeholders and the remaining part to John Jones. (This probably accounts for the many smallholders still in Cerney).  It remained in the Jones family for 200 years. It then passed to the Suttons, thence to the Biddeles, on to Cromwell Hockley who sold it to the present writer. Sir Robert Atkyns probably wrote his great book on Gloucestershire in this house.

On a window pane the following inscription is cut:‑ “ A chaise horse named, Sharper, remarkable for inoffensiveness of temper and a favourite of his master, John Jones Esq., by whom he was kept above 25 years, was buried in the court beneath this window on March 17 1789 aged 32.” The bones were found when alterations were made in 1906.

There are various names of Jones cut on the old glass in the house. One of the John Jones was a miser and deaf and dumb. £50 was found in gold in the panelling in a room which is now a bathroom. No doubt this was the miser’s money. A portion of this panelling was used for the new screen by the South Door of the Church. The Manor House, facing the main street, is now called South Cerney Manor and is occupied by Captain E. T. Cripps, M.C.

 

WYE MANOR

The most valuable. This manor was bestowed by Margaret de Bohun on the Priory of Lanthony, near Gloucester in 1287. On the dissolution of the religious foundations the manor was granted to William of Southampton, then to William FitzWilliam, Earl of Southampton. It passed to Francis Wye in 1556 hence the name of this Manor. From the Wyes it went to the Jones family who also held the Atkyns Manor and who retained it for several generations.

This manor is now called The Manor House, and is owned and occupied by Col. C.W.G. Walker, C.M.G., D.S.O.  It is the house next to the Church.  

CHAPTER MANOR

On the establishment of the Dean and Chapter of Bristol the King endowed them with this manor, hence sometime called “ The Chapter Manor.”

In 1674 the lessees were the family of Dewe. A hundred years later it passed to Thomas Bush who built a handsome modern house. It is said that the house was built from stone from a building, the remains of which are still standing at the end of the garden. This building is reputed to have been a chapel. It must have been of considerable size as the foundations were found 3ft. below ground over a considerable area adjoining.

In an extract from the Dean and Chapter of Bristol dated 1830 it is referred to as the mansion house of the Manor of South Cerney. In 1834 it was referred to as South Cerney House. Mr. H. Whittaker Cantrell, who bought in 1911, renamed it South Cerney House, it having been known for many years as the Manor Farm. The present owner occupier is Lt. Col. R T. W Glynn, M.C. The house lies East of the Church.

EDWARDS’ COLLEGE

Mrs. Ann Edwards was the daughter of Thomas Hayward of Compton Casey. Her mother was Widow House of Bibury, a sister of John Jones who owned and lived at Atkyns’ Manor and Possessed considerable property in the Parish. Mr. Hayward, her father, died in 1785 in financial difficulties.  After her father’s death, mother and daughter (Ann) resided at the Manor House near the Church. She continued to live there with her mother until the latter died in 1813. In 1802 Ann married the Rev. Isaac Edwards, then curate of the Parish during the incumbency of the Rev. Anselm Jones. At first the young couple were poorly off until Mr. Edwards inherited money from various relatives, eleven of  whom died in one year (1815).  So at her husband’s death Mrs. Edwards was well off. Mr. Edwards died in 1819 from lung trouble contracted from sitting in wet clothes at the Bishop’s visitation in Cirencester Church. Mrs. Edwards’ half sister, Mrs. Middleton, started litigation which lasted in the Courts for five years until a compromise was reached in the Court of Chancery.

After her husband’s death Mrs. Edwards’ first act was to pay off all the bankruptcy debts of her father, and in addition handsomely paid £700 accrued interest for 43 years to his creditors. She paid for a clock for the Church and for Embroideries. She built the National School (£1,500) and the School House (£1,000) and  £1,000 for the poor of South Cerney in charities. The residue under her will was to be for the relief of widows and orphans of distressed clergymen in the Diocese of Gloucester.

Two years after her death in 1834 the Trustees decided to erect a permanent memorial to her and built what is now called ‘ Edwards’ College.’ Mr. Jones gave the site and two houses were built called ‘Jones’ Lodging’ and ‘Cerney Lodging’.  These are now Nos. 6 and 7 of the present range of houses; and the other houses were added later.  The architect was Mr Piers St. Aubyn, who was also responsible to the restoration of the Church.  The building is in the Gothic style in Kemble Stone and is 240 feet long.

After being managed for many years by a local committee, the College has been taken over by the Diocesan Board of Finance who are now responsible for its upkeep.

 

THE SCHOOL

This was built in 1820 at the expense of Mrs. Edwards. At the time it consisted of only, one room the East end of the present building. In 1860 the large room adjoining was added, and subsequently. an infants’ room and cloakroom.

As already stated, Mrs. Edwards also built the School House, in 1830.

CUTTS CHARITY

The history of this Charity is probably one of the oldest in England.  In 1425 Cutts and his wife lived in this village. Their tomb lies in the churchyard on the West side of the Church porch with the figures of Cutts and his wife in carved stone.  The stonework is, considering its age, in fair preservation as it is thought that originally it was within the Church before being removed to its present position. The woman’s head is covered with a veil, which corresponds with the fashion of that date. All the deeds, some 32 in number, were found in a box at a farm in Cerney Wick, under the bed of the farmer. They are written in the ancient Latin and were sent to the Bodleian at Oxford to be translated and transcribed.  Every deed since the time of Henry VI has been preserved and deposited in the Record Office at Gloucester.  The first deed, written on parchment in ancient Latin dated 1425, is hung in the vestry of the Parish Church.  It describes the property as I Vergate of land and a messuage. Which consisted of the old George Cottages, now pulled down, adjoining the Chapel, Luker’s Stores, originally 5 cottages, and Clark’s Hay, the field adjoining  ( so called as a man named Clark lived there), and other fields.  It is interesting to note that the field still retains the same name as it did in far distant days centuries ago.

 

The first mention of a managing body of persons, probably the original trustees, appears in a deed dated 1501, and the first description of the objects and distribution of the Charity was in 1602, they were as follows :‑“1/3rd, to the support of the poor men and women in South Cerney, I/3rd for the repair of the King’s Highway, I/3rd for the repair of the Parish Church.” The distribution of the Charity at Christmas is the same to this day, except that the Highway portion is now paid to the Rural District Council for the relief of the rates.

Some 40 years ago the Charity Commission drew up a scheme, authorised the then trustees to sell the property as the cost of upkeep and repairs left little for the beneficiaries, and invest the money in gilt edged securities. This was done, and the income of the Charity was more than doubled.

THE CANDLELIGHT AUCTION.

By ancient custom the property was let by auction on seven year leases.  In those days the auctioneer’s hammer had not been thought of and, until the property was sold, the ancient custom of letting it was done by light of a candle, one inch long, the last bidder before the candle burnt out became the tenant. This was no easy job for the Chairman of Trustees who conducted the proceedings, as may easily be imagined. The School in which the auction was held was pitch dark except for the light of one small Christmas tree candle. As the candle began to flicker before it burnt out the bidding became fast and furious. The bidders were seated some distance away as the slightest puff of breath at the right moment would extinguish the expiring flame and ensure the bidder being accepted. This ancient custom has ceased to exist now that the Charity Commission scheme has taken its place.

There is a photograph of the last auction by candlelight, though unfortunately it cannot be reproduced in this book.

THE VILLAGE HALL

Among the social institutions in the Parish, the Village Hall is probably the most useful and one that has filled a much needed want.

After the 1914-18 War, South Cerney decided that a village hall was necessary and it was one of the first villages to erect a village hall of its own.

A central building was essential, and one worthy of its surroundings.  This at the moment was not forthcoming.  In the hopes of fulfilling this desire Capt. E.T. Cripps offered as a free gift the old Tithe Barn in School Lane with its yards and outbuildings, he having first obtained the assistance of Mr. H. Collicutt (and no better craftsman can be obtained for a job of this kind) to cover the barn with a thick covering of thatch.  This form of roofing has proved itself excellent for the purpose as it is warm in Winter and cool in Summer.  The excellence of the work can be seen after 25 years, when it was first put on.  It takes a certain amount of imagination to realise what can be done to convert a more or less derelict building into a village hall.

Before accepting the gift, the Committee were instructed to view all possible sites in the village.  None were found suitable or obtainable for the purpose so the proposed building was accepted.

The village meeting decided that two principles should be laid down, that no donations should be asked for, that two thirds of the cost should be provided by hard work and one third by a loan.  Both these admirable suggestions were carried out to the letter.  £200 was loaned by the Rural Community Council, and so successful was the hall financially that this debt was paid off in two years, leaving the Hall Committee free of debt.

The drawings and design of the hall were made by Mr. S. Gambier Parry, Architect, and the work carried out by Mr.  R.A. Berkeley under the supervision of Mr. G. Cox.  Both the architect and the builder generously agreed to do the work on a non-profit basis.

Gas and electric light were installed, and later a permanent stage was erected, resulting in a most successful dramatic society, the South Cerney Players, being started, who have for years given dramatic performances, a source of pleasure to players and audience alike.  A billiards table was presented by Mr. H. Cantrell, and the hall was decorated with exceptionally fine sporting trophies presented by Col. C. Elwes of Colesbourne, some of them are records for size and beauty.

With the wholehearted assistance of the ladies of the village by fetes, bazaars and entertainments, the money was raised and the work finished.  On May 25th the hall was opened by Lady Suffolk.  Much use and enjoyment has proved how right was the decision to provide a hall.

The village was complimented by the Rural Community Council who reproduced the two photographs on the front page of their annual brochure inscribed :‑“ What can be done with all old barn, before and after.”

Some years after its opening the South Cerney hall was selected by the Council for their film on Village Life.  100 people from the village were employed on the different films who had never realised before what hard and continuous work film production is to both operators and actors. Scenes were filmed of all the activities that took place in the hall as well as the building, Country Dancing, Dramatic Society, Whist Drives, and Roof Thatching. Many of our ladies became film stars before a strenuous fortnight came to an end. It is interesting to note that our village hall and life has been screened in the Colonies anti Dependencies for propaganda purposes.

Many of the performers will remember with pleasure the team of operators and their amazing patience, and above all Miss Grierson the producer, who spent two months inl the village preparing details or the various scenes, and with sadness when the news of her death by enemy action in the Atlantic shortly afterwards was received.

The hall is now used in the daytime as a canteen for schoolchildren’s meals in addition to the social use made of it in the evenings.

VILLAGE CROSSES

From the South to the North you will find crosses, Market Crosses, Preachers’ Crosses, Memorial Crosses.

These wayside crosses were not erected without a purpose, and the earliest have a religions significance. Many of these crosses are now headless columns; this has been due partly to the wear and tear of the weather, but more is due to the ill usage during the mid-seventeenth century civil wars.  Some are fortunate in having been preserved.

Many of the crosses were primarily erected for the purpose of preaching when Churches were few and people could only be reached by going out into the highways. The raised steps provided a platform for the preacher, and the cross was the emblem of his message.

As to our own village crosses, it would seem probable that the old one is about 500 years old. The new one, erected to the memory of those who gave their lives in the 2 Great World Wars, lies further West, and is inscribed With the names of the fallen and the following inscription:-

“Sons of this place let this of you be said

That you who live are worthy of your dead

These gave their lives that you who live may reap

A richer harvest ere you fall asleep.”

 

 

 

SOUTH CERNEY PARISH CHURCH

Sir Robert Atkyns in his invaluable book on Gloucestershire tells us that there was a priest here in 1086, and presumably a chapel in which he ministered.  Experts who have visited the Church agree that there are evidences of an earlier and smaller church; their inspection of a good deal of masonry now preserved in the tower belfry supports this view of an earlier building.

The ecclesiastical records date from 1100 when the Advowson with the Tithes was given by Walter (son of Earl Roger) a Sheriff of Gloucester, to the monks of the Abbey of St. Peter of Gloucester. In 1327 it was appropriated to the Abbey by Adam de Orleton Bishop of Worcester, who settled the allowance to the Vicar. After the dissolution of the monasteries the Rectory and advowson were granted to the Bishop of Gloucester in 1542, and the Bishop is still the Patron.

The Church is dedicated to All Hallows and has examples of the various styles of architecture, Pre‑Norman Norman, Early English, Decorated and Perpendicular.  The earliest part is the tower, the arches are late 12th century, an example of the first use of the pointed Norman arch.

The tower was originally surmounted by a spire, which was struck by lightning in 1855 and was taken down. A subscription list was opened to restore the damage which was estimated at £55. £50 was received, and it is sad to relate that for lack of £5 the spire was taken down. The beautifully worked stones of the octagonal spire were stored in the churchyard, where they remain to this day. Ancient books on Gloucestershire describe this spire as “ a mark of beauty in the countryside.” The tower walls were also damaged, but owing to their thickness. 5ft., survived for many years till the rebuilding of these walls was undertaken in 1912.

Fortunately there lived in the village a craftsman in stonework, Frederick  Cox, who was also the Church Clerk and who had the interests of the work at heart. Single handed and without any help lie rebuilt the walls. The South side was completely taken out in pieces and rebuilt. Evidence of the excellence of his work remains in the  rebuilding of’ the lancet windows,  the belfry stairway, the repair of the columns. Some of the rolls of the columns had been plastered up, hiding the beautiful original work. This he carefully removed, exposing the lovely Norman arches and columns. All this work was carried out by Fred Cox during three or four years. The only exceptions are the two heads at the ends of the rolls of the arch, a crowned head with palm branches, representing All Hallows, and a head representing Abbot Parker, the last Abbot of’ Gloucester and, as some believe, the last Rector of the parish. These two heads are not his work.

In the North wall of the tower is a recess, carved by Cox, for the reception and preservation of an antique relic, the Head and Foot from the figure on the Cross, which were found by the writer and Cox in a square hole in the wall, in which the support to the Rood rested, which had been plastered over with three irregular stones. Cox put his arm into the hole up to the elbow and reached the head and foot, handing them to a small child, Peggy Cripps, who was watching him, with the remark “ Be very careful, that’s something valuable,” little realising he had found a relic almost unique in England as will be seen from the Antiquarian report which follows The spot is now marked by an incised cross at the back of the pulpit. This incised cross is one of the many dedication or consecration crosses found in the tower walls during restoration.

It is not improbable that the remainder of the carved and painted wooden figure may still be hidden in the walls of the Church, perhaps placed there for preservation at the time of the Reformation. This relic consists of the Head and Foot of the figure of Christ on the Cross from the rood screen; the relic is of foreign origin and was probably, brought to this country about 1200 A.D. All that remained intact was the painted shell, the rest was powdered wood, decayed by age. It was carefully, preserved and filled with sawdust and glue to prevent all fear of expansion and damage to the outside shell.

The Head and Foot were sent to the Antiquarian Society who had heard of the find and wished to report on it. It was photographed by the Victoria and Albert Museum, and has been treated at intervals with a preservative similar to that used in the Tutenkhamem finds in Egypt, and which has been entirely successful.

The following is the report of the Antiquarian Society:‑ “ This relic is, as far is we know, the earliest piece of painted wood carving in the country. It formed part of the 12th century Rood; the whole figure was probably 3ft. high, and it would therefore have appropriated a 6ft. Cross. The Eyes are closed, the Head leans forward the Hair arranged in long tresses carried back over the crown of the head; an Oriental type is represented. It closely resembles the Christ in Winchester Cathedral, a work of 1180 A.D., and the earliest painted Crucifix at St. Albans, A.D. 1200.”

The following poem was written by Mrs. Owen Jenkins who, at the time of the discovery, was living in the village.

THE RELICS

Long ago in the dark years

When the learned were few indeed.

There came to be placed in Cerney Church

A sign ‑ that all could read.

 

A Rood, carved by some Eastern hand,

The figure of God that bled

On the weary Cross with closed Eyes

And a patient bended Head.

 

Perhaps some old crusader knight

As he passed through a white walled town

Had paid to the fashioner pieces of gold

To call the thing his own.

 

Perhaps he had brought it over the sea

To teach his churls to pray:

For though they could not read or write

They might learn of Christ this way.

 

Six good candles before the Rood

Glimmered on Holy days

As through the grey Church arches flowed

The tide of prayer and praise.

 

Godfrey was only a poor man,

He could neither read nor write,

But he knew the tale of the Sacrifice

And the names or’ the Saints so bright.

 

He would come to the door of Cerney Church

And kneel with cap in hand;

The latin was hard to follow, but

The Cross he could understand.

 

In his ignorant humble life

He thought over and over this thought.

“ If Christ is in me, as the preacher saith,

I can work as He has wrought.”

 

And His patience ever before me goes

In the fields if I close my eyes

! can see him hanging in Cerney Church

And remember the Sacrifice.”

 

Strange things befell as the years went by,

‘Twas trouble that sin doth bring.

Instead of kings fighting for Holy Church

‘Twas the Church fought with the king.

 

Abbot and priest and prior and nun,

They’ve had their little day.

Their ways of worship are over and done –

Let us do as the king’s men say.

 

The king’s men came to Cerney Church

They broke the glass and the stone,

They rifled the treasure, despoiled God’s house,

Then passed, and left it alone.

 

In the moonlit dawn of a Winter’s day

When the cock‑crows rang in the chill

Godfrey came to the gaping door

And his sorrowful heart stood still.

 

For the wanton soldiers had broken all

With many a venomous jest,

They had stripped the glass and the carved wall

And the Rood had gone with the rest.

 

Among the heaps of stone and dust

With shaking hands he sought

And out of that second Calvary

The Head and the Foot he brought.

 

He kissed the Head, he kissed the Foot,

With his tears he washed the twain;

Then thrust them into the broken wall

Where the beam would ne’er rest again.

 

Through the long years, indifferent years,

The Holy Relic lay

Till the spirit of reverence was stirred

In the calm of a latter day.

 

And the care restored its dignity

To the ancient Church again,

And the mason rebuilding the old grey wall

Found Godfrey’s treasures twain,

 

There in the Church they rest once more,

A Sign to passing eyes

Of the Love that is shown from age to age

For that Love that never dies.

 

Above the Western Arch of the tower, facing the centre aisle, is a recessed doorway, now walled up; all that remained of the original doorway were the left hand jamb and sill; the whole was restored from stones found buried in the filling of the tower walls. It may have been connected with the Rood. It may have been connected with the Rood loft, and used by the congregation so that they could participate in the services held in the Chance! beyond the Rood Screen.

The Priest’s door in the South Wall of the Chancel is a delightful example of 14th Century work decorated with Ballflower and Ogee Arch. This door was completely walled up and was exposed by F. Cox. An oak door was fitted.

A mass of beautifully carved arch stones, columns and dog tooth work was found in the same way, many of them marked by fire, probably part of a Saxon or early Norman Church destroyed. They have been built into the East wall of the belfry to preserve them. A printed notice is hung there to inform future generations the meaning and reason of this museum of carved stones, and what their history is.

Two Saxon stone coffin lids were also found buried in the tower wall, with Bishops’ Crosses on them.  One is now placed in the chancel and one by the South door.

The South doorway is probably Anglo‑Saxon work in origin the columns and arch are enriched with strap work and heads of animals. The Bath Antiquarian Society believes that the doorway was “ probably removed from an earlier Church on the site.”

Above this doorway is another unique feature, a Tympanum, believed to be Saxon work. In the upper part, within a Vesica, we see our Lord with His right hand uplifted in blessing. The lower part is more difficult to decipher as the stone is much decayed by age. It is thought by the Antiquarian Society that it is the Harrowing of Hell; some think it may be the Expulsion from the Garden of Eden; it is very indistinct and we must leave it at that. In Ely Cathedral there is a better example of the upper part, but it is of later date than ours.

The small lancet window on the left of the North door has some beautifully coloured pieces of glass. It is said that a South Cerney man who was working on Fairford Church brought home each Sunday night small pieces of the fine glass for which Fairford is renowned.

The East wall of the Sanctuary shows the remains of frescoes; no doubt the whole wall was originally treated in this way.

The sculptured head with the abbott’s mitre in the chancel sedilia is thought to be that of Abbott Parker, last Abbott of Gloucester, referred to above.

There are two Piscina, the one in the side chapel is 13th century, Early English. The corner one in the Sanctuary with its Credence and wonderfully carved canopy is later, Decorated.

The Font is of good 14th century design.

There are five Dedication or Consecration Crosses, two on the North wall of the tower, 2 on the South wall of the Tower, and one over the pulpit, all very old, not later than the 14th century. Dedication crosses were customary

from Saxon times. At the Dedication ceremony of a new Church one or more crosses were made, or, in some cases, a new aisle or Chantry Chapel.  Consecration crosses were used if any part of the Church had been defiled by a murder or other heinous offence; a solemn office or repurgation and consecration was necessary before the Church could be used again.

 

THE REBUILDING OF THE CHURCH IN 1861/62

Rudder describes the Church as follows :‑ “ The Church contains a nave, an aisle on the North, with a semi‑transept and in the middle a low tower and spire. The whole structure is early Norman style.”

The body of the Church was practically all pulled down with the exception of the old stone pillars on the North side of the nave (13th century work).

There is an entry in the Churchwardens’ book dated 1861 stating :‑“ Started pulling down the old Church”. Another July 1, 1862:- “The new Church opened by the Bishop of Gloucester and Bristol.”

The Chantry Chapel was done away with (to be replaced by the Holy Trinity Chapel 75 years later in 1936). The South wall removed to its present position and replaced with pillars to match the existing ones on the North side of the nave, thus forming the South aisle and absorbing the Chantry Chapel The North wall of the Church was probably rebuilt on the same site as the work and windows are new work, absorbing the semi-transept.  The two galleries at the West end were removed, the West wall was rebuilt, enlarging the Church and adding an extra pillar and arch space to the length. The roof and roof timbers were stripped, new timbers and tiles replacing the old roof’ and new seating was installed. The architect was Mr. Piers St. Aubyn who had previously built the College.

In 1936, as mentioned above, the Holy Trinity Chapel was added. It occupies the site Of the 14th century Chantry Chapel of which the only remaining portions are the piscina and the tracery of the window over the Altar.

The restoration was effected by the labour and gifts of Parishioners and friends.

A beautifully illuminated book in the Chapel Sanctuary enumerates the names and gifts of the donors. The Chapel was dedicated and the Altar, Vessels and Ornaments consecrated on Trinity Sunday of the same year by the Lord Bishop of Gloucester, Dr. A. C. Headlam.

Of the two galleries at the West end which were done away with. one accommodated 100 girls, and the other a choir of 30 persons, and no doubt it was in that gallery the musicians were seated. During the Commonwealth (1644) it was enacted that all organs should be taken away. Fiddlers, either alone or with other instruments, supplied the music. That was at the time of the ‘ Box ‘ pews and three‑decker pulpits.

In early days seats were not thought necessary as worshippers knelt or stood at the services. For the aged and infirm stone seats we’re sometimes placed against the wall. The stone seats in the belfry were removed when the tower was rebuilt. This may be the origin of the saying “The weakest go to the wall”.   Stalls were provided in the Chance! for the singers and ministers. Fixed seats in the body of the Church came into use about the end of the 14th century, and during the 16th became almost general. At first they were a board, and later were provided with a back.  The ends were sometimes richly carved.

At the beginning of the 18th century a new form of seat appears, that was the time when people went to Church to hear long sermons, and bodily comfort was more considered. The nave was divided into spaces with panelling four or five feet high, and within these ‘box’ pews seating was arranged on all four sides, and people entered through doors of the same height. Portions of the old box pews are still to be found in the old cottages. Pews were allotted to houses rather than to individuals and would pass to successive occupiers of such houses. Other people sat at the back wherever they could.

CHURCH LIGHTING AND HEATING

It is evident that up to the beginning of the 19th century services were in daylight, as in medieval times, when the only exception was the Winter Mass. At first there were candles, succeeded in turn by oil lamps, in 1907 gas, and electric. The last was installed in 1931.

Until the beginning of Victoria’s reign Churches were apparently unheated. In our Church radiators were put in in 1909 in place of a hot air system.

CHURCHWARDENS

The office of Churchwarden was instituted as long ago as the Council of London in 1127. Their duties originally were to raise and administer funds for the maintenance of the Church fabric and the services and to prosecute offenders against Ecclesiastical law. They were the legal guardians of property belonging to the Church and Parish and were responsible for parish accounts.  After the Reformation many civil duties were also put upon them, such as the provision of arms for the militia, relief of maimed Soldiers, the destruction of vermin, etc. They received no pay for all their work and if, when engaged upon their official duties, they had good meals and took copious draughts of ale at the Parish’s expense, they deserved it.

Many changes were made by the Enabling Act in 1921.

The writer occupied the office of Warden for 43 years, and when he retired in 1949 he was presented with a copy of Atkyns’ great book on the county, a book written in the house he now occupies. A presentation was also made to Mr. Henry Luker, who had held office as Churchwarden for 25 years and for 20 years as a sidesman, retired at the same time. Mr. Luker died a few months later at the age of 82.

CHURCH CLERKS

The office is an ancient one and before the Reformation the clerk was often a sub‑deacon or even a deacon, sometimes he was studying for the priesthood. In the middle ages he taught the children singing and gave them a simple schooling. One of his duties was to go through the Parish at certain Festivals carrying Holy Water with which he sprinkled the people. In the 18th century he sat in the lowest seat of the three‑decker pulpit.

The writer has known many Church Clerks. The one who stands out among them is Fred Cox who did such good work in the restoration of the Church.

THE CHURCH CLOCK

The clock in the South wall of the tower was given by Mrs. Edwards at a cost of £130 in 1820 It has kept remarkably good time for over 100 years. Originally the bearings of all the working parts were of oak. A few years ago the clock was rebuilt in a steel frame, with metal bearings, ensuring another century or two of good service to the inhabitants. The St.George flag, originally flown on Buckingham Palace, was presented to the Church by the Royal Navy.

THE SCRATCH DIAL

These dials are very common, there are at least 130 in this county alone. Their object was to mark the time of the services. What they originally looked like we do not know, rain and re‑limewashing having removed all the painted lines. The Scratch Dial on the south side of the Tower was photographed in 1935 but weather has now ruined all trace of it.

The coming of mechanical clocks, the first in England was constructed in 1370, displaced the mediaeval system. The majority of the dials we see to‑day were probably made in the 14th and 15th centuries.

It was a useful way of finding the time, anyone could make such a simple time‑keeper by merely driving a long nail into a board and placing it due South.

They served their purpose and are now relegated to oblivion.

 

THE CHURCHYARD

In early times it was common for fairs and markets to be held in the churchyard, in spite of efforts to stop them At the e end of the 13th century an act was passed prohibiting this, but it continued here and there much later. In the old Churchwardens’ account book there is an item in 1806 for a ‘Padlock for the Church gate.’ In the early 19th century the churchyard gate was only unlocked for services on a Sunday.

In 1942 the question of enlarging our own churchyard had to be faced unless a public cemetery was provided. Col. C. W. G. Walker generously gave a piece of ground in the Little Berries so that the existing burial space could be extended eastwards.

Mrs. Cantrell provided in her will that further extensions to the South in the Downings could be made if and when the Church Council required it. This portion was taken in during 1950 and enclosed by a stone wall built by Mr. R. Stait.  This ensures sufficient burying ground for another 100 years.

THE OLD VICARAGE

This was rebuilt by the then Vicar, the Rev. Alexander Saunders, (a very forceful character he was too!) about 1705 or 1707. The present vicarage, according to Miss Hadow, was originally the Police Station. The Rev. W. H. Liddell made some alterations, and the Rev. H. E. Hadow who followed him made additions for his pupils.  Sixty five years ago the writer and his brother used to drive over from Ampney Park in a donkey cart to do schooling with Mr. Hadow in their holidays.

CHURCHWARDENS’ ACCOUNT BOOKS

It is regrettable that we have nothing earlier than 1845, but these supply items of interest The Churchwardens as already mentioned, had no means of recovering their expenses of duty, but they were able to charge comfortably for their meals, £1 for the two of them.

The bellringers do not appear to have been paid, except for special occasions ; e.g. in 1852 for the funeral of the Duke of Wellington; for the celebration of peace at the Crimean War in 1856, and the marriage of the Prince of Wales in 1863.

One item occurs with remarkable regularity, viz. new bell ropes. In  the 9 years searched there were seven years when new bell ropes were bought from which one may infer that either there was an enormous amount of bell ringing, that the ropes were of poor quality, or, perhaps uncharitably, they were discarded more readily inasmuch as worn out bell ropes became the property of the Churchwardens
