A NOTE ON THE BUILDING OF ANN EDWARDS COLLEGE, SOUTH CERNEY

 

By Ann Turner 1999

 

Ann Edwards died on the 11th August 1834, but due to the challenge to her will made by her half-sister, Sybil, Mrs.Middleton, a final settlement was not reached until 1837.  After personal legacies and £1000 left to the poor of South Cerney, Mrs. Edwards had willed the remainder of her estate to trustees to provide a college for the widows and orphans of indigent clergy.  It was to be built on the edge of the Berries, on land donated by her first cousin, Thomas Jones.

 

Headed by the Rev. Mr. Richard Wilbraham Ford, the trustees chose as designer a young man of 29, Thomas Fulljames (1808-1874).  He was already Surveyor to the County as well as to the Diocese, and the following year was to become a Fellow of the Royal Institute of British Architects.  The earliest of his drawings for the College are dated 1837, and the latest is for the memorial to Mrs. Edwards in All Hallows Church of 1839.  There are several early drawings showing a terrace of 6 houses, and a variant for 8 houses, as well as for the final design of 12.  In 1835 Fulljames had considered entering for the competition to design the new Houses of Parliament, and among the prints he owned were views of Hampton Court Palace.  This may have influenced his design for an elaborate 8 house version of the College which has a great deal of decoration and oriel windows.  In the end a plainer Tudor style, similar to that of many Cotswold vernacular buildings was adopted.

 

Fulljames was meticulous, not only in drawing out the plans, but also in giving precise instructions to masons working on the decorative details, including foliage, coats of arms, the profile of buttresses and the crisp script of the Latin inscription in the central gable.  The gates and fencing were designed to be imposing.  In the composition of the elevation he is obviously interested in the play of light – one drawing had dramatically emphasized shadows – to enliven a façade which, because of its length, could have become a monumental block.

 

The interiors were designed with equal care, from the problems of plumbing and access to backyard pumps, to the charming detail of chimney pieces and the practicality of the folding shutters which work very well to this day.  The Specification of 1838, which must mark the start of the building, emphasizes the need for care and supervision from a hut built on the site, as well as the quality of the materials to be used.

 

It is obvious no expense was spared to create a sympathetic home for these genteel families down on their luck.  However, it obviously took some time to build.  The 1851 Census has inhabitants only in No’s 1,2,9 and 10; the rest of the houses are either uninhabited (8,11 and 12) or ‘not finished building’ (3-7), so there must have been a considerable noise and dust from the continuing work for some years.

 

Ann Turner 1999

ANN EDWARDS COLLEGE, SOUTH CERNEY:

 

 

LATIN INSCRIPTION ON THE CENTRAL GABLE

 

HOC COLLEGIUM QUOD INDIGENTIUM CLERICORUM

VIDUAS ORBASQUE RECIPIAT, ET MUNERIBUS

ANNAE ISACCI EDWARDS OLIM PASTORIS HUJUS

ECCLESIAE, BIDUAE, SUPERAGRUM A THOMAS JONES

ARMIGERO LARGE CONDONATUM ET STRUCTIM EST.

AD MDCCCXXXVIII

 

 

Translation

 

This College, which provides a retreat for the widows and orphans of indigent clergy, and the gift of Ann, widow of Isaac Edwards, formerly priest of this parish, is founded and built on land generously given by Thomas Jones, Esp.

AD 1838

 

 

 

 

       

 

 

 

                                                           

 

Inscription on the Memorial to Ann Edwards, South Cerney Church

 

This

Monument

Is erected to the memory of

Ann

Relict of the late

Rev Isaac Edwards MA

Formerly Curate of South Cerney

Who died on the 11th day of August 1834

In the 57th year of her age

She was nearly allied to the ancient and

Respected family of Jones

Principal proprietors of the soil in this parish

Her benevolence has seldom been surpassed

How great was her public and open beneficence

The following plain statement will evince but

The extent of her private charities however will

Never be disclosed till that day in which

The secrets of all hearts shall be revealed

When her works of

 

Liberality  Benevolence  and  Integrity

 

Will follow her through the merits

Of that redeemer in whom she trusted

And whose example of doing good

She humbly endeavoured to imitate

In 1820 she gave L1500 into the hands of

Certain trustees to establish and endow a

National school in this parish

And presented also a new clock to the church

At a cost of L130

As well as richly embroidered hangings to the pulpit

And reading desk to the amount of L40

In 1828 she gave an additional L1000 to build

Houses of residence for the master and mistress

Of the national school

By her will she left in the hands

Of trustees L1000 to the poor of South Cerney

The interest of which is to be distributed yearly

At their discretion on St Thomas’ Day

In bedding clothing bibles and prayer books

She also left the residue of her property

After having given L500 each to the Gloucester Infirmary

And society for promoting Christian knowledge

To the widows and orphans of distressed clergymen

Of this diocese out of which ample fund

The neighbouring college

Called in gratitude after her name was erected

By the bishop and clergy in 1838

For the reception of the afflicted families of indigent ministers
