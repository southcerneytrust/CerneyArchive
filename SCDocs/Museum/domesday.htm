South Cerney Exhibition


After the Charter, Cerney passed to the monastery at Abingdon. Fifty years later it was leased by Bishop Stigand who, to the monks' distress, failed to give it back!

 

In 1066 William the Conqueror gave the manor of Cerney to his close ally, William FitzOsbern.

 

By 1086, the time of Domesday, FitzOsbern was dead and the manor was held by the de Pitres family. Curiously a tiny corner, about 120 acres near Cerney Wick, had become detached and joined to a manor at Ampney where it stayed for over a century.

 

 

At Domesday Cerney had three mills, worth 30 shillings per annum, and Cerney Wick had another. The manor had a priest, 25 villeins, nine bordars and four serfs with, altogether 12 plough teams. Cerney Wick had 21 slaves. Most notably there was over  100 acres of meadow. Good pasture was rare and Cerney's lush acres were highly prized. This was why Stigand kept them and why the monks tried to regain the manor. It is also why the de Pitres' descendant, Miles of Gloucester, could afford his private army.
