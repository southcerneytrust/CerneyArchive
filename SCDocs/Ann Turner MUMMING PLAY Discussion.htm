THE SOUTH CERNEY MUMMING PLAY: TWO VERSIONS OF THE TEXT

By Ann Turner

 

1.       The Oxford Treasury of English Literature (1906-8)  Vol. IIPs 287-294:-

 

The editors of this “companion to the study of Shakespeare” were Grace E. Hadow (1875-1940), and her eldest brother William Henry Hadow (1859-1937), children of the vicar of South Cerney, Rev. W.E. Hadow, who would have seen the play performed during their childhood.  The last performance was at Christmas 1913.  In their preface acknowledging sources for the various plays they reproduce in the anthology, they state that “the Mumming play was supplied to us by the actors themselves, from traditional parts orally transmitted”.

 

They include an introduction (P287) describing the tradition of these plays in English villages, and descriptions of its performance, costumes and props in South Cerney.  The text, as they point out, “has been much corrupted on oral transmission”, and they have cut sections as “later accretions” in their version, mainly the part of Beelzlebub/Jack Vinny, as can be seen by comparison with version 2.

 

2.       Typescript;  South Cerney Mumming Play  from Raymond Fenton, Atkyns Manor, South Cerney:-

 

Peggy Fenton (1906-1979 – nee Cripps) of Atkyns Manor would also have seen some of the last performances of the traditional play.  In the late 1950s she gathered 3 or 4 of the surviving Mummers round her dining-room table with a large supply of beer, and transcribed whatever they could remember of the play.  Though several of the characters are elided, this is a longer text although there are inevitable further corruptions as well.  There is no evidence that Mrs. Fenton was aware of the Oxford Treasury version.  2 performances were given of this text. The first circa 1959 and the second to a village social circa 1964.  The costumes made under Mrs. Fentons supervision consisted of brightly coloured check trousers and loose fitting jackets, except for the doctor who wore a black frock coat and black trousers, and carried a Gladstone bag for his bottle of magic pills.  The Fool, Jack Vinny, had a blown-up pigs bladder (courtesy of the Cirencester butcher Jesse Smith) tied to a light stick.  Raymond Fenton who played this part, says the inflated bladder when it dried out was quite hard in texture.  The principal actors fought with long wooden staves.  The musician plays whatever instrument he is familiar with and at the 1964 performances he was Bruce Wakefield with a clarinet.  All the costumes are now (1999) stored in the attic of Atkyns Manor.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Version 1

 

 

 

 

ROBIN HOOD AND THE TANNER

 

A Mumming Play

 

Dramatis Personae

 

 

The Tanner of Nottingham                                                Farmer Jack

Robin Hood                                                                      Tinker Tom

Little John                                                                        Father Christmas

The Doctor                                                                      Beelzebub

Fool

 

Tanner.  Give room. Give room. This gallant hall,

     And give me room to ride:

And I will show you an activity

     On this merry Christmas-tide.

Activity of life,

     Activity of age,

Such an activity never seen

     Or acted on a stage.

I am bold Tanner from Nottingham,

     My name is Arthur `a Brand;

There is not a squire in all Hampshire

     That dares to let me stand.

With my long spike-staff on my shoulder

     See how well I clear my way:

With one, two, three, I make them all flee,

     And give them more labour to stay.

As I was walking through the forest,

     Bold Robin Hood did I spy.*

 

As soon as he did me spy,

     He thought some sport for to make;

He stole out his hand, and bade me stand,

     And thus to me he spake.

Robin Hood.  Who art thou, bold fellow,

     That standest so boldly here?

Sure, and in brief, thou look’st like a thief,

     Come to steal away the king’s deer.

Tanner.  I am the keeper of the forest,

     The king put me in trust,

To view the red deer, that run here and there,

     And stop thee, bold fellow, I must.

Robin Hood.  If thou be the keeper of this forest,

     And have such great command,

Thou must have more in store than thou had’st before,

     Ere thou biddest me to stand.

Tanner.  I have no more to be taken in store,

     No more than I have need;

I have a staff and another old graff**

     And I’m sure it will do its deed.

Robin Hood.  For thy sword nor thy bow do I care not a strow,

     For all thy brave boasts to boot,

If thou once gets a snap all on the brain-top,

     Thou might’st as well fly as shoot.

Little John.  What is the matter, master, pray?

     I pray unto me tell:

I see thee stand with a staff in thy hand,

     And I fear it is not all well.

Robin Hood.  It’s a man and a man, and he bids me to stand,

     And a Tanner he stands by my side:

He’s a bonny blade and a master by trade,

     And he swears he’ll tan the hide.

Little John.  If it’s to be commanded by that

     It’s as much as he can do;

If he’s so stout he and I’ll have a bout,

     And I’ll tan his hide well too.

Tanner.  Pray, and let us measure our staves

     Before we begin our fray;

I would not have my staff Ben longer than thine.

     For that would be foul play.

Little John.  My staff is out full length,

     My staff is out full glee;*+

My (trusty) staff will knock down a calf,

And I’m sure it will knock down thee.

Tanner.  Let us this bout begin, and I’ll see

     If I can’t make a better man of thee.

 

(Fight begins; The Tanner for a moment gains the advantage.)

 

Little John.  What, dost thou think, thou proud fellow,

     That thou hast conquered me,

I’ll let thee see before I go

     I’ll fight before I’d flee.

 

(Fight resumed: Little john wounds the Tanner)

 

Little John.  Doctor, doctor, where dost thou be?

     The bold Tanner’s wounded in the knee.

Five thousand pounds I’ll lay me down,

     If a noble Doctor can be found.

Doctor (without).  Not no more than that?

Little John.  Ten thousand pounds I’ll lay me down

     If a noble Doctor can be found.

Doctor (without).  Hold my horse, Jack: rack him up with a furze faggot, and give him a bucket as ashes to drink.  (Enters.

 

Good morrow, Ladies and Gentlemen all,

And a merry Christmas to you all.

I am a noble Doctor stout and good.

I can cure the purging of the blood,

The itch the stitch, the palsy or the gout,

All pains within and all pains without.

Bring me an old woman nine years dead, ninety-nine

     Years buried, a hundred years laid in her grave;

If she’ll rise up and crack one of my golden pills

I’ll be bound in a bond of fifty pound her life shall be saved.  So don’t believe me any longer, ladies and gentlemen: I can cure this man if he’s not quite dead.

(Gives a pill to the Tanner who is lying motionless>

I have travelled through England, Scotland, and France,

Rise up, bold Tanner, and let’s have a dance.

Tanner (reviving)  Terrible, terrible, the like was never seen:

     Enough to frighten a man out of eleven senses into seventeen.

 

Farmer Jack.  Here come I old Farmer Jack,

 Wife and family at my back;

Out of eleven I got but seven,

They, poor things, are gone to Heaven:

Out of seven I got but five,

They, poor things, were saved alive:

Out of five I got but three,

Where they be gone I shan’t tell thee.

Out of three I got but one

And he is gone to Burford++ stone.

Out of one I got but nane

And he is gone to Narlase# Farm.

Tinker Tom.  Here come I, old Tom the Tinkerd,

I was never a small-beer drinkard.

I told the Chandler to his face,

The chimney corner was his place.

Father Christmas.  Here come I old Father Christmas.

Christmas comes but once a year,

When it comes it brings good cheer,

Beef, plum-pudding, mince-pies, and beer.

Beelzebub.  Here come I, Beelzebub,

On my shoulders I carry a club.

In my hands a dripping-pan,

Am not I a funny old man?

Fool.Here come I, who haven’t been yet,

With my great head and little wit,

My head is great, my wit is small,

So I play a tune will please ye all.

 

(Here follows a dance of the characters.  The Fool plays, and Beelzebub, who takes part in the dance, strikes at the others with his bauble.)

GOD SAVE THE KING

 

 

Many of the mumming plays have come to include ‘moderns who have caught the popular imagination’: e.g. Bonaparte (Leigh) and the king of Prussia (Bampton), Nelson (Devon), and Wellington (Cornwall).  In one of the Wiltshire versions there is even a part for the Prince Imperial.  See Mr. Chambers, op. Cit., vol. i. p. 212.

Version 2

 

 

 

SOUTH CERNEY MUMMING PLAY

 

 

          Characters:            Bold Tanner of Nottingham

                                      Little John

                                      The Doctor

                                      Jack Vinney

                                      Robin Hood

                                      The Musician

 

 

 

                Enter Bold Tanner who walks round thumping staff on Ground

 

 

Bold Tanner               Give room, give room, this gallant hall,

                                    And give I room to rise.

                                    I'll show ye such activity this Christmas tide.

                                    Activity of life, activity of age,

                                    Such activity never seen or acted on a stage.

                                    I am Bold Tanner of Nottingham.

                                    My name is Arthur O'Brand,

                                    There's not a squire in all Hampshire

                                    That dare to bid I stand.

                                    With my long spike staff all on my shoulder

                                    See how well I clear my way.

                                    With a one, two, three, I make 'em all flee

                                    And give 'em more labour to stay.

 

                                    As I was walking through the King's forest,

                                    To view the red deer as runs 'yer and ther',

                                    There bold Robin Hood did I spy.

                                    Vain sport he thought to make.

                                    He stole out his hand, and forbad I to stand,

                                    And thus unto me did he spake    . . . . . .

 

 

                Enter Little John

 

 

Little John                  Who art thou bold fellow, that stands so boldly 'yer?

                                    Sure and in brief thou look'st like a thief

                                    Come in the night to steal away the red deer.

 

Bold Tanner               I am the [1]keeper of this forest and have such great command.

                                    The King put I 'yer, to view the red deer, that runs 'yer & 'ther.

                                    And stop thou bold fellow I must.

Little John                  If thou art the keeper of this forest, and 'ave such great command,

                                    Thou must have more in store than thou had'st before

                                    Thou bidd'st I to stand.

 

Bold Tanner               I have no more to be taken in store than I need,

                                    I have a staff and another old craft,

                                    Which I'm sure will do its deed.

 

Little John                  For thy sword and thy bow, I cannot a-strow,

                                    For all the brave Orris to boot.

                                    If thou once get a snuff all on the brave top

                                    Thou might just as well flee as to shout.

 

Bold Tanner               Pray let's measure our staves before we start this fray.

                                    I would not 'ave my staff 'alf a foot longer than thine,

                                    Or else that 'ud be faulty.

 

Little John                  My staff out full length, my staff out full glee,

                                    My staff will knock down a calf,

                                    And I'm sure 'e will knock down thee.

 

 

                They begin to fight with staves, bending low, tapping one end of staves on ground and then knocking other man's stave.  Bold Tanner getting the worst of it.

 

 

Bold Tanner               What dost thou think bold fellow, that's conquered me.

                                    I'll let thee see before I go, I'll fight thee afore I flee.

 

Little John                  Pray let this bout begin.

                                    This day I'll knock thee back'ards.

 

 

                They fight on.  Little John knocks Bold Tanner's knee and he falls.

 

 

Little John                  Doctor, doctor where bist thee, Bold Tanner's wounded in the knee.

                                    Five thousand pounds I would lay down

                                                if a noble doctor could be found.

 

Doctor (Outside)         What, no more 'an that?

 

Little John                  Yes, yes, ten thousand pounds I would lay down,

                                    If a noble doctor could be found.

 

 

                Exit Little John.

 

                Enter Doctor calling backwards as he comes in.

 

Doctor                         Hold me 'orse, Jack

                                    Rack 'im up with a furze faggot,

                                    Give 'im a bucket of cold ashes to drink.

 

                                    Good morning ladies and gentlemen all,

                                    A merry Christmas to you all.

                                    I am a noble doctor, both stout and good.

                                    With my created hand I can purge the blood,

                                    Cure the itch, the palsy and the gout.

                                    All pains within and without.

 

                                    Bring me an old woman ninety years dead,

                                    Ninety-nine years buried and a hundred years in her grave.

                                    If she can sit up and crack one of my golden pills,

                                    I'll bound a bond for fifty pounds that her life will be saved.

 

                Feels Bold Tanner's knee.

 

                                    So don't believe me any longer, ladies and gentlemen,

                                    I can cure this man if he's not quite dead.

 

                Rubs Bold Tanner's knee and gives him a drink.

 

                                    I've travelled through England, Scotland and France,

                                    Rise up, Bold Tanner and 'ave a dance.

 

 

                Exit Doctor.

 

 

Bold Tanner               Terrible, terrible life that was never known.

                                    Enough to drive a man out of eleven senses into seventeen.

 

                                    Walk in Jack Vinney.

 

 

                Enter Jack Vinney.

 

 

Jack Vinney               My name 'aint Jack Vinney.  My name be Mr. Vinney.

                                    A man of great property, more than thee or any other man.

 

Bold Tanner               Ah and very likely too.

 

Jack Vinney               Did I ever do 'ee any 'arm?

 

Bold Tanner               Ah, saucy Jack, therefore get thee gone.

 

Jack Vinney               Gone, kind sir, I 'ware thee stab.

 

Bold Tanner               Stab thee, sir, I do not care.

                                    The point the place, I'll meet thee ther'.

                                    Across the Grove, the hour o' five,

                                    I'll meet thee there dead or alive.

                                    Across the Grove, the hour o' six,

                                    I'll meet thee there when the hour be fixed.

 

Jack Vinney               Ah, poor boy, led astray,

                                    Swam nine miles, across, across, across.

                                    Split 'is 'ead open 'gainst the windmill post.

                                    Well shave, well shove,

                                    Pick my choice for supper,

                                    Fish, fowl or any other bird,

                                    Perhaps I may pick a bone.

 

                                    Last Christmas Eve I turned the spit,

                                    I burned my finger, I felt the heat.

                                    The heat stood up like handsome man,

                                    And swore 'e swopped the dripping pan.

 

Bold Tanner               Ah, says the gridiron, can't you two agree?

 

Jack Vinney               Yes, I'm just the head of the town: don't you know me?

 

Bold Tanner               Ain't thy name John May?

 

Jack Vinney               So I 'eard thee say.

 

Bold Tanner               Didn't thee get the maid in the barn?

 

Jack Vinney               Well I didn't think that any 'arm.

 

Bold Tanner               Didn't thee come to breed a rhyme?

 

Jack Vinney               Yes, me 'umble servant, I was just going to wish you good-night.

 

                                    As I was walking through a farmyard

                                    I tripped over Wimble Straw, fell over a barn into a pigsty,

                                    Pitched with penny loaves and thatched with candle sticks.

                                    I kicked at the maid and the door came out.

                                    Asked me if I'd 'ave a crust o' ale or a glass of bread and cheese

                                    I said, yes thank you, no, if you please.

                                    'yer comes I old Farmer Fat,

                                                the wife and family at me back.

                                    Out of eleven I've got but seven,

                                                and they poor things 'ave gone to heaven.

                                    Out of seven I've got but five,

                                                and they poor things are starved alive.

                                    Out of five I've got but three,

                                                and they be gone I sha'n't tell thee.

                                    Out of three I've got but two,

                                                and they be gone I sha'n't tell you.

                                    Out of two I've got but one,

                                                and 'e've gone down to Burford Stone.

                                    Out of one I've got but narn,

                                                and 'e's gone down to Nor' Lease Farm.

 

                                    'yer comes I old Tom the Tinker,

                                                never was so small a drinker.

                                    I told the Landlord to his face,

                                                the chimney corner was his place.

                                    'yer comes I old Cut and Slashum,

                                                Cut and Slasham is my name.

                                    I cuts 'em down as fine as dust

                                                and sends 'em to Satan to make pie crust.

                                    'yer comes I old Belsebub

                                                and on my shoulder I carry's a club.

                                    And in me 'and a drippin' pan

                                                and don't you think I'm a jolly old man?

                                    'yer comes I old Father Christmas.

 

                                    Christmas comes but once a year,

                                    And when it comes it brings good cheer.

                                    Roast beef and plum pudding,

                                    And little money in our pockets, Ladies and Gentlemen.

 

 

                Jack Vinney begins to go out.

 

 

Bold Tanner   Who's that comes over yonder green hill did I spy?

 

 

                Jack Vinney turns and speaks as he is going out.

 

 

Jack Vinney               Little John and Bold Robin Hood,

                                    The man that'll fight with thee in thy field.

 

 

                Exit Jack Vinney.  Enter Little John and Robin Hood.

 

Bold Tanner               Thou stay thy hand bold fellow and let this bout begin,

                                    For if he's so stout we will have a bout

                                                and I'll tan his old hide well too.

 

Robin Hood                Oh 'old your 'and, oh 'old your 'and,

                                                and let these quarrels fall,

                                    For here we get our bones all smashed,

                                                and for no cause at all.

 

 

                Enter Musician.

 

 

Musician                     Yer comes I as ain't been 'it,

                                                with my girt 'ead and little vit  [feet]

                                    My 'ead so big, my vit so small,

                                                I'll play you a tune as 'll please you all.

 

 

                Musician plays a tune while the rest walk round him, except Jack Vinney, who stands out of the circle and beats time with the bladder by hitting the others as they go round.


    [1]            Pronounced "Kipper"
