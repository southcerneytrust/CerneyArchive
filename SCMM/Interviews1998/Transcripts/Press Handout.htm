A MILLENNIUM SELF-PORTRAIT

SOUTH CERNEY & CERNEY WICK

999 - 1999

A film based on interviews with 40 people linked to the Parish. It is divided into three sections as follows:-

I PARISH ROOTS:

The meadowland: small holdings and country crafts.

The creation of lakes in the Water Park through gravel extraction.

The activities of the Water Park: conservation and recreation.

The Church: it’s history and place in the village today.

The 150th anniversary of the consecration of Cerney Wick Church

in 1998.

The work of builders in the village.

The three Manor Houses: The Manor House, Atkyns Manor and

The Chapter Manor.

The gifts of Ann Edwards: teaching in the old school remembered;

the College, a refuge for "Widows and orphans of indigent clergy".

The creation of the South Cerney Trust to preserve the character of

the village.

II THE PARISH IN WARTIME:

The South Cerney Camp, originally built as a training station for the RAF in 1937, and now the base of the 29 Regiment, Royal Logistics Corps, lies within the Parish boundaries. Reminiscences of life in wartime are framed by sequences of the parade each year on Armistice Day when the Regiment joins the Royal British Legion and villagers in a Church service and march to the War Memorial.

III VILLAGE LIFE:

This section records the many activities of the parish: clubs and societies, shops, pubs, the school, the changes in the lives of children, sports, the rapid growth in housing and problems related to it, policing the area – in the old days and now, entertainments, the Flower Show, medical services and the Village Appraisal.

Apart from short sections on the Cotswold Water Park and on the bypass outside Cerney Wick, the entire film was shot within the boundaries of the parish.

Credits

A film by Raymond Fenton and Ann Turner

Raymond Fenton has lived in South Cerney all his life. Apart from his work as an accountant and musician – he has played the Church organ and trained the choir – he is a keen film-maker, and a director of Dream Team Television Ltd who supplied the equipment. On the film he combined the work of producer, cameraman, sound recordist and film editor, and was largely responsible for contacting the people interviewed, many of whom he had known since childhood.

Ann Turner is a former producer and director with BBC Television and retired in 1986. She has lived in South Cerney since 1981. Her credits include "Civilisation", Alastair Cooke’s "History of America", "Royal Heritage", and films on the lives of individual artists, including one about J.J. Audubon, the American naturalist, with Sir David Attenborough. She was responsible for the interviews, and their reduction to manageable sequences.

Commentary spoken by Raymond Thilthorpe who lives in South Cerney and is a former manager and commentator for the Red Arrows.
